Albumin is the principal determinant of the AG (~2.5 mEq per g/dL). Hypoalbuminaemia (present in up to 90% of ICU patients) lowers the AG and can mask an unmeasured anion — hence the corrected AG: AGc = AG + [2.5 × (4.5 − albumin)].

The delta gap & osmolal gap (Eq. 31.16 / 31.17)
Delta gap (gap-gap)

AG excess / HCO₃⁻ deficit = (AG − 12) / (24 − HCO₃⁻)

  • Ratio ≈ 1 — a pure high-AG metabolic acidosis.
  • Ratio < 1 — the HCO₃⁻ fall exceeds the AG rise → co-existing normal-AG (hyperchloremic) acidosis (e.g. saline-diluted DKA).
  • Ratio > 1 — the HCO₃⁻ fall is less than the AG rise → co-existing metabolic alkalosis.
Osmolal gap

Calculated Osm = 2 × Na⁺ + Glucose/18 + BUN/2.8 (+ Ethanol/3.7 if measured)

Normal osmolal gap (measured − calculated) is < 10 mOsm/kg H₂O. An elevated gap in a high-AG acidosis, especially without a lactate or ketone explanation, suggests an occult unmeasured osmole — methanol, ethylene glycol, D-lactate, or pyroglutamic acid.

Low-ECV / low-urine-Cl⁻ ("chloride-sensitive") alkalosis is the usual ICU pattern and responds to saline + KCl; high-urine-Cl⁻ ("chloride-resistant") alkalosis does not — volume-loading is not the fix and the underlying mineralocorticoid excess must be treated.
Saline dosing (Eq. 33.2/33.3)

Cl⁻ deficit (mEq) = 0.2 × wt (kg) × (100 − plasma Cl⁻)  →  Volume of isotonic saline (L) = Cl⁻ deficit ÷ 154

Hydrochloric acid infusion, for severe alkalosis (HCO₃⁻ > 50 mEq/L or pH > 7.55) (Eq. 33.4)

H⁺ deficit (mEq) = 0.5 × wt (kg) × (plasma HCO₃⁻ − 24)  →  Volume of 0.1N HCl (L) = H⁺ deficit ÷ 100, via central vein, ≤0.2 mEq/kg/hr

Ventilatory compensation is sluggish: ΔpCO₂ = 0.7 × ΔHCO₃⁻ (Eq. 33.1, same as the metabolic-alkalosis row above) — hypercapnia (pCO₂ > 46) needs the HCO₃⁻ to rise ~10 mEq/L, and worrisome hypercapnia (> 50) only appears once the alkalosis is severe. Neurologic symptoms (confusion, seizures, carpopedal spasm) are classically taught for alkalosis but are usually a feature of respiratory, not metabolic, alkalosis. Check magnesium before/while replacing potassium — diuretic-induced hypokalemia can resist K⁺ replacement if Mg²⁺ is also depleted.
